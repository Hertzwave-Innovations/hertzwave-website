Carbon Accounting 101

A complete primer on enterprise carbon accounting — understand Scopes 1, 2, 3, select the right data hierarchy, perform calculations, and prepare audit-ready disclosures.

10 min read · Updated Sep 2025

What Is Carbon Accounting?

Carbon accounting is the structured process of quantifying greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions across your organization and value chain. It allows enterprises to measure their climate impact, comply with disclosure regulations (BRSR, CSRD, SEC), and design decarbonization strategies.

Scopes Explained

Most frameworks follow the GHG Protocol which divides emissions into three scopes:

Scope 1 — Direct Emissions

  • Stationary combustion: boilers, furnaces, gensets
  • Mobile combustion: company-owned vehicles, construction equipment
  • Fugitive emissions: refrigerant leaks (R134a, R410a), SF₆ from switchgear

Scope 2 — Indirect Electricity

Electricity, heat, steam or cooling purchased from utilities. Reported in two ways:

  • Location-based: grid average emission factor
  • Market-based: adjusted for PPAs, RECs, green tariffs

Scope 3 — Value Chain

Upstream + downstream emissions, often 70-90% of a company’s footprint. Key categories:

  • Purchased goods & services
  • Capital goods
  • Fuel & energy-related activities (not in Scope 1/2)
  • Business travel, employee commuting
  • Upstream transport & distribution
  • Use phase & end-of-life treatment of products

Data Hierarchies

Good practice is to follow a “best available data” hierarchy:

  1. Activity data × specific EF (most accurate)
  2. Supplier-specific emission factors
  3. Industry averages from databases (Ecoinvent, DEFRA)
  4. Spend-based estimates (least preferred)

Sample Calculation

Scope 1 (Diesel)
Activity data: 12,000 litres
Emission factor (DEFRA 2024): 2.54 kg CO₂e / litre
Total emissions = 12,000 × 2.54 = 30.48 tCO₂e

Controls & Assurance

To ensure audit readiness:

  • Maintain versioned emission factors with sources and year
  • Track data lineage: where each number came from
  • Apply variance analysis vs prior periods
  • Implement internal review workflows before submission

Common Pitfalls

  • Mixing location- and market-based Scope 2 figures
  • Ignoring upstream Scope 3 categories (esp. capital goods)
  • Double counting renewable PPAs across scopes
